Different types of depression

When someone mentions depression, usually the first thing someone thinks of is the symptoms found most commonly in Clinical Depression. A common misconseption is that there is only one type of depression when in fact there are many, and here is a small list of the most common types of depression.

Clinical Depression

Major Depressive Disorder (MDD), also known as Clinical Depression is a severe form of depression that involves low mood and/or loss of interest in usual daily activities that were once enjoyable. To be properly diagnosed, you must experience symptoms most days for over two weeks.

Causes

Usually, there is no single cause for Depressive Disorders, however there are common causes among individuals diagnosed. A short list of causes includes:

• Stressful events

If you experience a stressful event such as a relationship breakdown, there is a higher risk of becoming depressed, especially if you close yourself off from family and friends and attempt to deal with the problem in question by yourself.

• Personality

Specific personality traits could make you more vulnarable to Depressive Disorders. Low self-esteem and extremely self-critical behaviour could lead to a depression.

• Family History

If there is a family history of diagnosed depression or depressive symptoms, it makes you more likely to experience depression.

• Alcohol and/or Drugs

Alcohol and drugs affect the chemistry of the human brain, which increases the risk of depression. Trying to cope with negative emotions through drugs and/or alcohol has the possibility of a spiral of depression.

• Illness

There is a high risk of depression if you experience a longstanding and/or lifethreatening illness such as cancer or cystic fibrosis. A head injury, both major and minor, could trigger mood swings, unstable emotions, and emotion problems in general.

Symptoms

• Feelings of sadness, tearfulness, emptiness or hopelessness
• Angry outbursts, irritability or frustration, even over small matters
• Loss of interest or pleasure in most or all normal activities, such as sex, hobbies or sports
• Sleep disturbances, including insomnia or sleeping too much
• Tiredness and lack of energy, so even small tasks take extra effort
• Reduced appetite and weight loss or increased cravings for food and weight gain
• Anxiety, agitation or restlessness
• Slowed thinking, speaking or body movements
• Feelings of worthlessness or guilt, fixating on past failures or self-blame
• Trouble thinking, concentrating, making decisions and remembering things
• Frequent or recurrent thoughts of death, suicidal thoughts, suicide attempts or suicide
• Unexplained physical problems, such as back pain or headaches

Bipolar Disorder

Bipolar Disorder, also known as Manic Depression is a disorder characterised by extreme shifts in mood, resulting in episodes of mood swings that range from manic highs to depressive lows.

Causes

The exact cause for Bipolar disorder is unknown, however experts have discovered a number of factors that contribute, including:

• Trauma

People who have experienced trauma in the past are more likely to develop Bipolar Disorder.

• Chemical inbalance

The neurotransmitters in the brain that control noradrenaline, serotonin and dopamine can become inbalanced, which is believed to be a cause of bipolar disorder.

• Genetics

It's believed that Bipolar disorder runs in families and is linked to the genetics of an individual.

Symptoms of mania (the highs)

• Excessive happiness, hopefulness, and excitement
• Sudden changes from being joyful to being irritable, angry, and hostile
• Restlessness
• Rapid speech and poor concentration
• Increased energy and less need for sleep
• Unusually high sex drive
• Making grand and unrealistic plans
• Showing poor judgment
• Drug and alcohol abuse
• Becoming more impulsive
• Less need for sleep
• Less of an appetite
• Larger sense of self-confidence and well-being
• Being easily distracted

Symptoms of Depressive periods (the lows)

• Sadness
• Loss of energy
• Feelings of hopelessness or worthlessness
• Not enjoying things they once liked
• Trouble concentrating
• Forgetfulness
• Talking slowly
• Less of a sex drive
• Inability to feel pleasure
• Uncontrollable crying
• Trouble making decisions
• Irritability
• Needing more sleep
• Insomnia
• Appetite changes that make you lose or gain weight
• Thoughts of death or suicide
• Attempting suicide

Types of Bipolar Disorder

Experts have found three specific types of Bipolar Disorder: Bipolar I, Bipolar II, and Cyclothymia.

• Bipolar I

Bipolar I is characterised by at least one manic episode, and a hypomanic or major depressive episodes before or after the manic episode. Bipolar I affects all sexes equally.

• Bipolar II

Bipolar II is characterised by a major depressive episode that lasts at least two weeks as well as at least one hypomanic episode that lasts roughly four days. Bipolar II is found to be more common in women.

• Cyclothymia

Cyclothymia is characterised by having shorter and less severe episodes of hypomania and depression. Most people with Cyclothymia experience no mood symptoms for one to two months at a time.

Postpartum depression

Postpartum Depression (PPD), also known as Perinatal Depression is a form of depression that occurs within a year following the birth of a baby. Although many women experience what is called "baby blues", it shouldn't last longer then two weeks. To be properly diagnosed with PPD, you must experience symptoms most days for over two weeks.

Causes

Although the cause for Postpartum Depression isn't clear, there are some factors that may either contribute or make you more vulnerable incuding:

• Hormones

Giving birth leads to a massive drop in the hormones estrogen and progesterone, which leads to mood swings, tiredness, anxiety, sadness, etc, which is the cause of baby blues. The drop of these hormones can also be the cause of postpartum depression if it lasts two weeks or longer.

• Self-image

Struggling with self-image is often a contributing factor to other depressive disorders, and with the combined stress of birth, a child, life can feel out of control and some struggle with their sense of identity.

• Family History

A history of depressive disorders in a family can contribute to experiencing depressive disorders, including postpartum depression.

• Stressful events

Birth and the first few weeks of parenthood is a stressful stage of life, and any added stress such as a recent break up or bereavement can be a cause of postpartum depression.

Symptoms

• Feeling sad or crying a lot
• Feeling overwhelmed
• Having thoughts of hurting the baby or yourself
• Not having an interest in the baby
• Having no energy or motivation
• Feeling worthless, guilty, or like you are a bad parent
• Sleeping too much or too little
• Change in relationship with food
• Feeling anxious
• Having chronic headaches, aches, pains, or stomach problems

Psychotic Depression

Psychotic Depression is the label given to a group of depressive disorders that affect the mind and thoughts, leading to hallucinations, delusions, etc.

Causes

Experts are yet to discover a single specific cause for Psychotic Depression, however they have discovered several possible causes, including:

• Environmental Factors

Experiencing trauma or stressful events in the past, especially in early childhood hightens the risk of all depressive disorders, including Psychotic Depression.

• Brain Chemistry

Dopamine and Seratonin imbalances often play a role in most mental health conditions, including depression, psychosis, etc. Some evidence also suggest that stress hormones also may play a part.

• Genetics

It's been discovered that some individuals are more likely to develop a form of depression if a first-degree relative (parents, sibling, or child) also has a depressive disorder.

Symptoms

• Hallucinations, or seeing, hearing, and feeling things that aren’t real
• Delusions, or believing things that aren’t true
• Psychomotor impairment, or slowed thoughts, movements, and feelings
• A state of stupor, where you’re unable to move, speak, or respond to your environment
• Believing you have a serious health concern, despite multiple tests showing otherwise
• you have unique or special powers
• Believing you’re a famous person or historical figure
• voices criticizing or mocking you
• Seeing a frightening or threatening animal following you
• Paranoia, or an irrational or extreme suspicion of other people

Psychotic Depression often is paired with MDD (Major Depressive Disorder), and in this case, the following symptoms can also be present:

• A persistent low, empty, sad, or hopeless mood
• Loss of interest and pleasure in activities you used to enjoy
• Sudden or unexplained changes in appetite and weight
• Sleeping difficulties, including sleeping much more or much less than usual
• Less energy than usual or lingering fatigue
• Changes in movement, such as increased restlessness or a sense of being slowed down
• Difficulty concentrating or making decisions
• Frequent feelings of worthlessness, helplessness, self-hatred, or guilt
• Frequent thoughts of death, dying, or suicide

Types of Psychotic Depression

There are many identified types of Psychotic Depression. This list includes:

• Schizophrenia

Schizophrenia is a mental disorder affecting an individuals interpretation on reality. Schizophrenia is known to last longer then six months, generally a year, and there is no known cure.

• Schizoaffective disorder

Schizoaffective disorder is a mental disorder that includes both Schizophrenia as well as a mood disorder such as MDD or Bipolar disorder. Although treatment can help ease symptoms, this disorder has no known cure.

• Schizophreniform disorder

Schizophreniform disorder is a mental disorder similar to Schizophrenia in sympmtoms however this type is known to last between one to six months. This is known to be developed in one in one thousand individuals within their lifetime.

• Brief psychotic disorder

Brief psychotic disorder is a mental disorder involving having one sudden, short period of psychotic behaviour. Recovery from this is quick, generally less then one month.

• Delusional disorder

Delusional disorder is a mental disorder characterised by having delusions generally lasting one month involving real-life situations such as having a disease.

• Shared psychotic disorder

Shared psychotic disorder, also known as folie à deux is a mental disorder where one or more individuals in a close relationship share delusions with one another.

• Substance-induced psychotic disorder

Substance-induced psychotic disorder is a mental disorder that is caused by the withdrawal of drugs and/or alchohol, which leads to psychotic episodes or psychotic disorder symptoms.

• Psychotic disorder due to another medical condition

Psychotic disorders can be caused by other medical conditions such as a traumatic head injury, brain tumor, etc. This can cause delusions, hallucinations, and other symptoms can be caused by another medical cause.

• Paraphrenia

Paraphrenia is a mental disorder similar to schizophrenia in terms of symptoms however the individual develops the disorder later in life. This condition mainly causes paranoid delusions.
